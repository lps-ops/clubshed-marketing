## Next three posts to write

### Post #3 — How to Apply for Football Equipment Grants in 2026 (UK Guide)

**Target keywords:** "FA grants for football equipment", "Football Foundation grants application", "grassroots football funding 2026", "Sport England Small Grants football"

**Search intent:** A club secretary or treasurer searching for "how to apply for football grants" is high-commercial-intent. They have a need (money for equipment), they're researching solutions, and they need a guide they trust. This post owns that moment.

**Why write this third:** Builds on the End-of-Season Audit piece. Natural follow-up: "I've done my audit, now how do I get money to fix what's broken?" Internal link from Post #2 will be strong.

**Word count target:** 3,500-4,000 words. This is the kind of post that should rank for years and become the definitive guide.

**Section outline:**

1. **The grant landscape in 2026** — quick map of what's available. Major grants: Football Foundation (Grow the Game, Pitch Improvement, Premier League/PFA Capital), FA Community Football Fund (administered by County FAs), Sport England (Small Grants, Movement Fund), local council schemes. Note: Germany and Spain sections too (Sportförderung from Landesverband, Federación Española grants).

2. **What grant reviewers actually want** — five things every successful grant application has: (1) evidence of need, not just a list, (2) a clear audit trail of existing equipment, (3) realistic costs from real suppliers, (4) demonstration that the club can absorb the money well, (5) safeguarding and inclusivity statements. Reference the audit report from Post #2 as the evidence layer.

3. **Anatomy of a Football Foundation Grow the Game application** — walk through the actual application step by step. Eligibility criteria, evidence required, the specific questions asked. Include sample answers (anonymised). This is the section that makes the post rank because nobody else does this depth.

4. **Anatomy of an FA Community Football Fund application** — same but for the FA's scheme. Different criteria, different evidence.

5. **Sport England Small Grants — when it's the right fit** — covers community sport more broadly, useful for clubs doing inclusivity work or new initiatives.

6. **Local council and community grants** — overlooked but often easier to win. Tips on finding your local grants. Mention London Marathon Charitable Trust as an example of niche schemes worth knowing.

7. **DACH and Spain section** — for German clubs, walk through Sportförderung from the Bundesland, plus DFB-Stiftung grants. For Spain, regional federations and municipal sports budgets.

8. **Common reasons applications fail** — vague evidence, unrealistic costs, missing safeguarding mention, applying for things outside the scheme's scope, missing deadlines. Each one with the fix.

9. **A timeline for the funding year** — month-by-month calendar of major deadlines. June: Grow the Game opens. July-September: many local schemes close. October-November: FA quarterly schemes. Etc. This is the content that gets bookmarked and returned to.

10. **Templates and examples** — a structured "what to include" template that the reader can copy. Link to the ClubShed audit report feature as the evidence layer.

11. **CTA** — ClubShed's audit report PDF generates the evidence layer automatically. Start free.

**EEAT signals to embed:**
- Real grant programme names with their actual eligibility criteria (verified against current sources)
- Specific funding amounts (e.g. "Grow the Game funds up to £1,500 for new teams over three years")
- The author's experience applying for grants at HVV Football Factory
- Cross-link to the audit guide (internal authority building)
- Last-updated date prominently displayed (grant programmes change, freshness signal)

### Post #4 — Spond vs ClubShed: When to Use Which (and Why You'll Probably Want Both)

**Target keywords:** "Spond alternatives", "Spond competitor", "Spond equipment tracking", "best grassroots football club software", "Spond vs"

**Search intent:** A club secretary already familiar with Spond who's looking for something different OR comparing options. High-intent commercial keyword.

**Why write this fourth:** Comparison content is rocket fuel for SEO. "X vs Y" pages convert at very high rates. The honest angle ("both, actually") differentiates from typical comparison posts that pretend their product is better at everything.

**Word count target:** 2,500-3,000 words.

**Section outline:**

1. **What Spond is great at** — be genuinely fair. Communication, fixtures, payments, member management. Acknowledge it dominates the market and works well for what it does.

2. **What Spond is bad at** — equipment tracking. Basic asset list bolted on. No QR codes, no condition tracking, no audit reports for grants. This is the specific gap.

3. **What ClubShed is great at** — equipment tracking, audit reports, seasonal logic, QR stickers, allocation across teams.

4. **What ClubShed isn't trying to do** — payments, fixtures, member management. Spond does these well, no reason to rebuild them.

5. **The honest recommendation** — use Spond for member admin, ClubShed for equipment. Total cost £15-20/month each, still cheaper than the larger all-in-ones.

6. **A decision tree** — "if you have < 30 items and don't lose much, stay with Spond's basic asset list. If you've lost more than €500 last season, add ClubShed. If you need grant funding, ClubShed's audit report is the killer feature."

7. **Side-by-side feature comparison table** — equipment-specific features only. Not trying to compare everything.

8. **Migration guide** — how to actually get your existing equipment data out of Spond and into ClubShed. CSV export from Spond (if available), CSV import to ClubShed.

**EEAT signals:**
- Author's experience using both
- Fair treatment of Spond (avoid the "we're better" trap, Google penalises this)
- Last-updated date
- Specific feature claims that can be verified

**One important note:** check Spond's actual current features before publishing. They iterate fast. Don't claim they don't do something they actually now do.

### Post #5 — Free Grassroots Football Inventory Spreadsheet Template (with How-To)

**Target keywords:** "football club inventory template", "free football equipment spreadsheet", "grassroots football inventory excel", "football club equipment list"

**Search intent:** Someone who hasn't reached the "I need software" decision yet. They want a free template. This post gives them one (a real, useful Google Sheets template), explains how to use it, and converts the most serious users into ClubShed signups when they outgrow the spreadsheet.

**Why write this fifth:** Lead magnet content. People search for free templates, download them, give their email. Builds the waitlist before the audience converts to paid users.

**Word count target:** 1,500-2,000 words. Shorter because the value is the template, not the prose.

**Section outline:**

1. **What this template gives you** — three sheets: Inventory (items), Loans Log (checkout history), Annual Audit. Each pre-formatted with formulas. Brief screenshots.

2. **How to use it** — five minutes from download to first entry. Specific instructions.

3. **The limitations** — be honest. Spreadsheets don't work on phones in kit cupboards. They don't scan QR codes. They don't send reminders. They don't generate grant audit reports. They're a good starting point, not a long-term answer.

4. **When to upgrade beyond the spreadsheet** — three signs: more than two teams, more than 30 items, losing more than €100 of gear per season.

5. **CTA** — ClubShed is free for two teams and 30 items. If you're outgrowing the spreadsheet, the free ClubShed tier is your next step. No card needed.

**The template itself:**

Build a real Google Sheets template with:
- Header row with proper data validation (categories from dropdown)
- A "QR ID" column (manually entered, but explained)
- Condition rating dropdown (1-5)
- Status dropdown (in storage / out / overdue / lost)
- Pivot table on the audit sheet that summarises by category
- Conditional formatting (red for overdue, amber for poor condition)

Host the template at a publicly accessible Google Sheets URL with "Make a copy" link, OR offer as an .xlsx download. Capture email before serving the file (this is the lead magnet mechanic).

**EEAT signals:**
- Real, working template (not a generic mock)
- Author's voice ("I built this for HVV Football Factory before I built ClubShed")
- Last-updated date
- Honest about limitations

## EEAT principles for every post

Apply these to everything written for the ClubShed blog.

### Experience
- Lead with first-person experience: "I've made this mistake" or "We learned this at HVV Football Factory."
- Concrete anecdotes beat generic advice. Specific amounts, specific dates, specific outcomes.
- If you haven't experienced it personally, don't fake it. Quote someone who has, or say "From conversations with other clubs..."

### Expertise
- Use the right technical terms (FA Charter Standard, County FA, accredited club, Pitch Improvement Fund, not vague descriptions).
- Cite specific grant programmes by their exact names.
- Use real prices in the relevant currencies (£, €).
- Name the actual products clubs use (Mitre, Nike, Decathlon, Forza).

### Authoritativeness
- Author byline on every post, with the credential (HVV Football Factory co-founder).
- Link to the /about page from every byline.
- Cross-link between posts to build internal authority.
- Update dates prominently displayed — content that's maintained signals authority.

### Trustworthiness
- /about page with founder identity, contact info, transparent backstory.
- hello@clubshed.pro email working and answered.
- Last-updated dates on every post.
- Be honest about ClubShed's limitations (e.g. acknowledging Spond does some things better).
- No exaggerated claims ("guaranteed to save you €1000" — instead, "clubs we've spoken to typically lose €400-€1000 per season").
- Real grant deadlines, verified at publish time.

### Voice rules
- No em dashes (—). Use plain hyphens, parentheses, or new sentences.
- No "I'd love to..." or "We're excited to..." openers.
- No "It's not X, it's Y" rhetorical constructions.
- No triadic lists used for rhythm ("we count, we audit, we report").
- Plain English. Volunteers are smart but tired.

## Posts to NOT write

These get suggested by SEO tools but don't fit:

- **"10 reasons grassroots football matters"** — generic, no buyer intent
- **"How to start a football club"** — different audience (people who don't have a club), too tangential to ClubShed's value
- **AI-generated listicles** — penalised by Google's spam updates, hurts EEAT signal
- **Bottom-of-funnel posts about specific features** — these belong on the product pages, not the blog
